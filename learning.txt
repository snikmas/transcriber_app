                                                                               
● Networking 101: How your request travels the internet                                      

  Every device has an address                                                                
   
  Just like every house has a postal address, every device connected to the internet has an  
  IP address.     

  Your laptop:     192.168.1.5   (local, inside your home)
  Your router:     109.45.23.187 (public, visible to the internet)

  When you open YouTube, they don't see your laptop — they see your router's IP. That's your
  identity on the internet.

  ---
  Where do IPs come from?

  Your ISP (Internet Service Provider — Kyivstar, Vodafone, etc.) owns a big block of IPs and
   rents them to customers.

  ISP owns:    109.45.0.0 → 109.45.255.255  (65,000 addresses)
  Gives you:   109.45.23.187  ← your home IP

  YouTube looks at 109.45.23.187 and can tell:
  - This is a residential IP (belongs to a real home user)
  - ISP is Vodafone Ukraine
  - City is roughly Kyiv

  This info is public — there are databases that map every IP range to its owner.

  ---
  What is a datacenter IP?

  Companies like Amazon, Google, Hetzner rent servers in data centers. Those servers also
  have IPs — but they're registered to Amazon/Hetzner, not to a home ISP.

  Home IP:        109.45.23.187  → owner: Vodafone Ukraine → looks human ✓
  Datacenter IP:  155.138.147.85 → owner: The Constant Company LLC → looks like a server ✗

  YouTube has a list of all known datacenter IP ranges. When a request comes from one —
  instant suspicion.

  ---
  What is a proxy?

  A proxy is a middleman. Instead of you going directly to YouTube, you ask the proxy to go
  for you.

  Without proxy:
  You (109.45.23.187)  →  YouTube
  YouTube sees: 109.45.23.187

  With proxy:
  You  →  Proxy (155.138.147.85)  →  YouTube
  YouTube sees: 155.138.147.85  ← proxy's IP, not yours

  The proxy fetches the page and sends it back to you. YouTube never knew you existed.

  ---
  Types of proxies

  Datacenter proxy — cheapest, fast, but YouTube recognizes and blocks them immediately. Your
   VPN uses this — that's why you had the timeout problem.

  Your request → Hetzner server in Germany → YouTube
  YouTube: "that's a server IP, suspicious"

  Residential proxy — the request comes from a real home connection. Much harder to block.

  Your request → Random person's router in Ohio → YouTube
  YouTube: "that's just some guy in Ohio, fine"

  But how does your request go through someone's home router? That person installed an app
  that sells their spare bandwidth. Companies like Bright Data, Oxylabs pay regular people a
  few dollars/month to route traffic through their connection.

  Regular person installs app → "earn money on your idle internet"
  Their router becomes a proxy node in a giant network
  Your scraper rents that node for $0.001 per request

  ---
  VPN vs Proxy — what's the difference?

  They're the same concept, different purpose:

  ┌────────────┬───────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
  │            │        VPN        │          Proxy           │
  ├────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ Purpose    │ Privacy, security │ Speed, bypass blocks     │
  ├────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ Traffic    │ All your traffic  │ Usually one app/protocol │
  ├────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ Encryption │ Yes               │ Not always               │
  ├────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ IP shown   │ VPN server's IP   │ Proxy server's IP        │
  └────────────┴───────────────────┴──────────────────────────┘

  Your VPN at 127.0.0.1:7890 — it's actually running locally on your machine, routing your
  traffic through some server abroad. That server has a datacenter IP → YouTube blocks it.

  ---
  The full picture of why download sites work

  Normal developer (you):
    Laptop → VPN datacenter IP → YouTube: "you're a server, blocked" ❌

  Download site:
    Their server → residential proxy (guy's home in Texas) → YouTube: "just a normal user" ✓

  They pay for residential proxy networks — costs roughly $3–15 per GB of traffic routed. For
   a site doing millions of requests, this is their main infrastructure cost.

  ---
  One sentence summary

  The internet identifies you by your IP address, IPs reveal whether you're a real person or
  a server, and proxies let you borrow someone else's identity — residential ones are trusted
   because they look like real humans.
